1. Foreword

1.1

Local Government Reorganisation provides a once-in-a-generation opportunity to redesign local public services around the needs of residents, businesses, communities and staff. For Stevenage Borough Council, this requires more than a technical response. It requires a clear, supportive and practical Enterprise Architecture operating model that helps services prepare for change while maintaining continuity and confidence.

This strategy sets out how a team of four Enterprise Architects will work together across Business Architecture, Data Architecture, Application & Digital Architecture, and Technology & Security Architecture. The purpose is not to place excessive responsibility on any one architect, but to create a collaborative environment where the architecture team can support each other, work closely with services and provide clear guidance to the LGR programme.

The strategy also explains how council services should be fitted together in a logical order. It starts with Customer Services and resident access, then moves through community, housing, place-based and regulatory services, before connecting to internal enabling services such as HR, Finance, Legal, Procurement, Democratic Services and technology foundations.

Thinking and justification: The foreword positions Enterprise Architecture as a practical support function rather than a technical control point. This matters because LGR will place pressure on staff, services and systems, and the architecture model needs to create confidence rather than add complexity.

2. Introduction

2.1

This strategy sets out the Local Government Reorganisation Enterprise Architecture operating model for Stevenage Borough Council. It explains how a four-architect team will work in a supportive, sustainable and service-led way to prepare for LGR, protect service continuity and shape the future authority.

Thinking and justification: The document deliberately starts with the operating model because the immediate need is not another technical catalogue; it is a clear way of working that protects capacity, gives services confidence and allows the architecture team to support LGR at pace.

2.2

The strategy applies to all LGR-related architecture activity, including business design, customer access, data, applications, digital services, technology, cyber security, responsible AI and corporate enabling services. It should be read alongside the Council’s Enterprise Architecture and Artificial Intelligence Strategy 2026–2030.

Thinking and justification: This scope makes clear that the strategy is not limited to IT. LGR affects services, people, data, processes, governance and residents; the architecture model therefore needs to connect all of those areas.

2.3

The strategy supports the Corporate Plan priorities by helping the Council design services that are accessible, efficient, financially sustainable and focused on resident outcomes. It also supports the move from isolated service design to a joined-up model that starts with Customer Services and flows logically through statutory, place-based and internal enabling services.

Thinking and justification: Starting with Customer Services reflects how residents experience the Council. Internal services such as HR, Finance, Legal and Procurement are then positioned as essential enabling capabilities rather than afterthoughts.

3. Legal Background

3.1

This strategy supports compliance with the legislation, standards and guidance that apply to local government transformation, public-sector technology and LGR transition activity.

The key legislation, standards and guidance include:

  • Local Government Act 1972.
  • Local Government and Public Involvement in Health Act 2007.
  • Data Protection Act 2018 and UK GDPR.
  • Freedom of Information Act 2000.
  • Equality Act 2010 and the Public Sector Equality Duty.
  • Public Sector Bodies (Websites and Mobile Applications) Accessibility Regulations 2018.
  • Procurement Act 2023.
  • NCSC cyber security guidance.
  • GDS Service Standard and Technology Code of Practice.
  • MHCLG Local Government Reorganisation policy, programme and implementation guidance.
Thinking and justification: The legal background is intentionally broad because architecture decisions during LGR can affect statutory service continuity, equal access, data protection, procurement, cyber security and public accountability.

3.2

The Head of Enterprise Architecture will ensure that relevant legislation and standards are reflected in architecture principles, design assurance, procurement requirements and transition roadmaps.

Thinking and justification: This makes compliance part of routine design activity rather than a separate check at the end of a project, reducing rework and implementation risk.

4. Equalities

4.1

Under the Equality Act 2010, the Council has a legal duty to fulfil the requirements of the Public Sector Equality Duty. Through this duty and in the application of this strategy, the Council will carry out its functions in a way that:

  1. Removes discrimination, harassment, victimisation and any other conduct that is unlawful under the Equality Act 2010.
  2. Promotes equal opportunities between people who have a protected characteristic and those who do not.
  3. Encourages good relations between people who have a protected characteristic and those who do not.

Further information on the Council’s fulfilment of the Equality Act 2010 is set out in the Equality, Diversity and Inclusion Policy 2022 and Reasonable Adjustment Policy 2024.

Thinking and justification: Keeping the standard wording protects consistency with the SBC corporate template. The architecture implication is that service redesign, digital access and AI-enabled change must be assessed for impact before implementation.

5. Data Protection

5.1

The Council regards respect for the privacy of individuals and the lawful and careful treatment of personal information as very important to delivery of services.

5.2

The Council will ensure that it treats personal information lawfully and proportionately as set out in the General Data Protection Regulation and Data Protection Act 2018. For further information on the Council’s approach to handling information please see Data Protection Act guidance on stevenage.gov.uk.

Thinking and justification: The data protection section is retained in line with the template. For LGR, the practical importance is high because migration, consolidation and service integration will involve resident, property, case, workforce and financial data.

6. Consultation and Coproduction

6.1

This strategy has been informed by engagement with the Enterprise Architecture function, Transformation and Digital, service leadership, Information Governance, cyber security, programme governance and emerging LGR planning activity.

Thinking and justification: The consultation base reflects the current stage of the strategy. As LGR decisions mature, wider engagement with services, staff, residents, members and partner authorities should be added to strengthen the evidence base.

6.2

The engagement has identified four key requirements:

  • The architecture model must be supportive and manageable for a team of four architects.
  • Services must be fitted together in a logical order, beginning with Customer Services and resident access.
  • Internal services such as HR, Finance, Legal, Procurement and Democratic Services must be treated as core enabling capabilities for vesting day readiness.
  • Architecture governance must help delivery teams make better decisions, not create unnecessary delay.
Thinking and justification: These findings directly shape the operating model. They prevent the strategy from becoming a theoretical TOGAF document and keep it focused on how people will work together during LGR.

6.3

Ongoing engagement will take place through service-cluster workshops, Architecture Design Authority sessions, programme board reporting and targeted reviews with corporate services.

Thinking and justification: Service-cluster workshops will make architecture visible and useful to services. This helps avoid the perception that architecture is only about systems or technical assurance.

7. Data Insight

7.1

LGR creates a significant data challenge because existing councils and partner services may hold overlapping, inconsistent or duplicated records for residents, properties, assets, cases, staff, suppliers and finances.

Thinking and justification: A clear statement of the data challenge helps senior leaders understand why architecture must begin early. If data discovery waits until system migration, the programme will inherit avoidable risk.

7.2

The architecture model will use master data domains to support joined-up service design. These domains will include Resident, Property, Organisation, Case, Asset, Employee and Finance.

Thinking and justification: These domains link the resident-facing front door to internal services. For example, a resident service request may depend on property data, case data, officer assignment, supplier activity and financial processing.

8. Key Objectives

8.1

The key objectives set out how the Enterprise Architecture team will support LGR while protecting capacity, creating a collaborative working environment and ensuring services are ordered coherently from resident access through to corporate enabling services.

Thinking and justification: The objectives are written as practical delivery commitments. Each one can be translated into work packages, architecture products and governance checkpoints.

Objective 1 – Establish a supportive four-architect operating model

The Council will establish four core architecture roles: Business Architecture, Data Architecture, Application & Digital Architecture, and Technology & Security Architecture. Each architect will lead their professional domain while working as part of a shared team, rather than operating as a separate silo.

Thinking and justification: This protects individual architects from becoming overloaded. Domain leadership gives clarity, while shared delivery ensures each major LGR decision benefits from business, data, application and technology input.
RolePrimary Accountability
Business ArchitectTarget operating model, service design, business capabilities, customer journeys, process architecture and change impact.
Data ArchitectMaster data, data quality, information governance, reporting, lineage, migration and analytics foundations.
Application & Digital ArchitectApplication portfolio, CRM, ERP, digital services, portals, APIs, integration and rationalisation.
Technology & Security ArchitectCloud, infrastructure, networks, identity, cyber security, resilience, end-user compute and technical standards.

Objective 2 – Organise LGR architecture around logical service clusters

LGR architecture activity will be organised around five service clusters. These clusters will create a logical order from the resident front door through to operational services, corporate services and shared digital foundations.

Thinking and justification: The service-cluster model makes the work easier for services to understand. It also stops the team designing HR, Finance or technology in isolation from the resident journeys and statutory services they support.
OrderService ClusterExamples
1Customer, Access and Resident ExperienceCustomer Services, contact centre, website, CRM, complaints, FOI, assisted digital and channel strategy.
2Community, Housing and People ServicesHousing, homelessness, community safety, safeguarding interfaces, voluntary sector and health partnerships.
3Place, Environment and Regulatory ServicesPlanning, building control, waste, environmental health, licensing, parking, estates and regeneration.
4Corporate and Enabling ServicesHR, Finance, Payroll, Procurement, Legal, Democratic Services, PMO, performance and risk.
5Technology, Data, AI and Shared PlatformsIdentity, integrations, data platform, reporting, cyber, M365, ERP, infrastructure and AI governance.

Objective 3 – Create an end-to-end service value chain

The Business Architect will lead the creation of a service value chain that connects resident need, customer contact, eligibility or identity checks, case creation, service delivery, finance, reporting and continuous improvement.

Thinking and justification: This gives the future authority a practical map of how work flows. It helps identify where delays, duplicate handoffs, data gaps and system weaknesses may affect residents or staff.

The service value chain will follow this sequence:

  1. Resident need arises.
  2. Customer contact is received through the appropriate channel.
  3. Identity, eligibility, location or account details are confirmed.
  4. A request, case, transaction or referral is created.
  5. The responsible service assesses and fulfils the request.
  6. Finance, payment, enforcement, procurement or workforce activity is triggered where required.
  7. The resident is updated and performance data is captured.
  8. Learning is fed back into service improvement.

Objective 4 – Align applications and digital services to the service clusters

The Application & Digital Architect will map applications, digital channels, integrations and supplier dependencies against each service cluster. This will support rationalisation, procurement planning, system consolidation and safe transition.

Thinking and justification: This prevents the programme from making premature product decisions. The right question is not simply which system to keep, but which systems best support the target service model, data architecture and vesting day requirements.

Objective 5 – Establish common data foundations for LGR

The Data Architect will define the common data model, master data domains, migration principles, data quality measures and reporting foundations needed to support the future authority.

Thinking and justification: Data is the thread that connects customer access, statutory services and corporate support. Without common data foundations, the future authority may inherit duplicated records, inconsistent reporting and weak resident insight.

Objective 6 – Define shared technology, cyber and identity foundations

The Technology & Security Architect will define the shared technical foundations needed for LGR, including identity, cyber security, cloud, networks, endpoint devices, collaboration tools, disaster recovery and operational resilience.

Thinking and justification: A new authority cannot operate safely without shared foundations. These decisions need to be made early enough to support staff onboarding, system access, information sharing and service continuity.

Objective 7 – Maintain AI as a cross-cutting assurance concern

Artificial Intelligence will be managed as a cross-cutting architecture and governance concern. AI initiatives will be reviewed through the existing Enterprise AI Architecture Board, drawing on Business, Data, Application & Digital, Technology & Security, Information Governance and EDI input.

Thinking and justification: This avoids creating a fifth overloaded domain while still giving AI the scrutiny it needs. Responsible AI depends on joined-up assessment of value, ethics, data quality, security, equality and public trust.

Objective 8 – Embed architecture into programme delivery

Enterprise Architecture will be embedded into LGR programme governance, business cases, gateway reviews, procurement activity and service design workshops.

Thinking and justification: Architecture is most useful when it shapes decisions early. Embedding the team into delivery governance reduces late-stage assurance issues and helps projects move faster with clearer standards.

9. Governance

9.1

This strategy will be governed by the Head of Enterprise Architecture, reporting through the Executive Director – Transformation & Digital and the relevant LGR programme governance arrangements.

Thinking and justification: Clear ownership is essential because LGR decisions will cut across services, systems, data, workforce, finance and political governance.

9.2

The following governance structure will apply:

Governance ForumPurposeFrequency
Weekly EA Team Stand-upReview workload, risks, decisions and service-cluster priorities.Weekly
Architecture Design AuthorityReview cross-domain architecture decisions and resolve design issues.Fortnightly
Technical Design AuthorityProvide detailed assurance for complex technology, integration, cyber and infrastructure designs.Weekly / as required
Enterprise AI Architecture BoardAssess AI initiatives for business value, ethics, data, application fit, security, equality and transparency.Monthly / as required
Architecture Roadmap ReviewReport progress, risks, dependencies and decisions to senior leadership.Monthly / quarterly
Thinking and justification: The governance model is deliberately layered. It separates day-to-day workload management, cross-domain architecture decisions, detailed technical assurance and senior roadmap oversight.

9.3

Architecture Decision Records will be maintained for significant LGR decisions. These records will capture the decision, options considered, rationale, implications, risks and review date.

Thinking and justification: Decision records are essential in a politically complex LGR environment because they provide transparency, auditability and continuity when membership, leadership or programme assumptions change.
